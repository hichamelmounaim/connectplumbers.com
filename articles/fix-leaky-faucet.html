How to Fix a Leaky Faucet in 30 Minutes

Stop that drip for good. A step-by-step walkthrough for the most common faucet types.

A dripping faucet is more than just annoying — it is wasteful and expensive. A faucet that drips once per second wastes more than 3,000 gallons of water per year. That is the equivalent of 180 showers, straight down the drain. The good news is that fixing a leaky faucet is one of the simplest plumbing repairs you can do yourself, usually in 30 minutes or less with basic tools.

Before You Start: Turn Off the Water

This is the single most important step. Before touching anything, locate the shut-off valves beneath the sink and turn them clockwise until they stop. Then open the faucet to release any remaining water pressure. If your sink does not have individual shut-off valves, you will need to turn off the main water supply to your home.

Once the water is off, plug the drain with a rag or sink stopper. Small screws and parts have a remarkable talent for falling directly into open drains.

Identify Your Faucet Type

The repair method depends on which type of faucet you have. There are four main types:

  • Compression Faucet: The oldest design. Has two separate handles. When you tighten the handle, a rubber washer presses against a valve seat to stop water flow. Leaks are almost always caused by a worn washer.
  • Ball Faucet: Single handle that moves over a rounded ball mechanism. Common in kitchens. Has multiple internal parts (springs, seats, O-rings) that can wear out.
  • Cartridge Faucet: Can be single or double-handle. Uses a cartridge that slides in and out to control water flow. Leaks usually mean the cartridge or its O-rings need replacing.
  • Ceramic Disc Faucet: The most modern and durable type. Uses two ceramic discs that slide against each other. Rarely leaks, but when it does, the disc cylinder or seals need attention.

Tools You Will Need

  • Adjustable wrench
  • Phillips and flathead screwdrivers
  • Replacement washers, O-rings, or cartridge (bring the old parts to the hardware store to match)
  • Plumber's grease
  • Allen wrench set (for some handle screws)

Fixing a Compression Faucet

  1. Remove the decorative cap on the handle (pry gently with a flathead screwdriver).
  2. Unscrew the handle screw and pull the handle off.
  3. Use an adjustable wrench to unscrew the packing nut beneath the handle.
  4. Pull out the stem. At the bottom, you will see a rubber washer held in place by a brass screw.
  5. Replace the washer with an exact-size replacement. Apply plumber's grease to the new washer.
  6. Inspect the valve seat (the brass ring the washer presses against). If it is rough or pitted, it may need to be replaced or resurfaced with a seat grinder tool.
  7. Reassemble in reverse order. Turn the water back on and test.

Fixing a Ball Faucet

Ball faucets have many small parts, so the easiest approach is to buy a complete ball faucet repair kit (available at any hardware store for around $15–$20). The kit includes replacement springs, valve seats, cam washers, and O-rings.

  1. Remove the handle (usually held by a small set screw under the handle, accessible with an Allen wrench).
  2. Remove the cap and collar using adjustable pliers (wrap the pliers in tape to avoid scratching the finish).
  3. Remove the cam, cam washer, and ball.
  4. Use needle-nose pliers to remove the old rubber seats and springs from inside the faucet body.
  5. Install the new springs and seats from the repair kit.
  6. Reassemble with the new cam washer and cap.
  7. Replace the O-rings on the faucet body (cut the old ones off, coat the new ones in plumber's grease).

Fixing a Cartridge Faucet

  1. Remove the handle and any decorative pieces.
  2. Remove the retaining clip (a small U-shaped metal clip holding the cartridge in place).
  3. Pull the cartridge straight out using pliers. Note its orientation — it must go back in the same way.
  4. Replace the O-rings on the cartridge (or replace the entire cartridge if it is damaged).
  5. Coat the new O-rings in plumber's grease and reinstall.

When to Call a Professional

If you have replaced the internal parts and the faucet still leaks, the problem may be a corroded valve seat, a cracked faucet body, or incorrect water pressure. At that point, a licensed plumber can diagnose the issue quickly and recommend whether a repair or full faucet replacement makes more sense.

Prevention Tips

  • Do not overtighten handles — this is the number one cause of premature washer wear.
  • Install a water pressure regulator if your home's pressure exceeds 80 PSI.
  • Replace aerators annually to prevent mineral buildup that stresses internal components.

A leaky faucet is not something you need to live with. With a $5 repair kit and 30 minutes of your time, you can fix it yourself and save hundreds in plumber fees.
