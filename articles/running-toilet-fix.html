How to Fix a Running Toilet

A running toilet wastes thousands of gallons a year. Fix it in under 20 minutes.

If your toilet continues to run long after flushing, or if you hear the tank randomly refilling on its own (known as "phantom flushing"), you have a running toilet. It is one of the most common and most wasteful plumbing problems in any home. A single running toilet can waste up to 200 gallons of water per day — that is over 6,000 gallons per month, and potentially hundreds of dollars on your water bill.

The good news is that diagnosing and fixing a running toilet is genuinely easy. In almost every case, the problem is caused by one of three inexpensive, replaceable parts inside the tank.

How a Toilet Works (30-Second Version)

Understanding the fix requires a basic understanding of how a toilet tank works:

  1. You push the flush handle, which lifts the flapper (a rubber seal at the bottom of the tank).
  2. Water rushes from the tank into the bowl, flushing waste down the drain.
  3. As the tank empties, the flapper falls back down and seals the tank.
  4. The fill valve opens and refills the tank with fresh water.
  5. A float (either a ball on an arm or a cup on the fill valve) rises with the water level and shuts off the fill valve when the tank is full.

A running toilet means one of these components is not doing its job. Let us find out which one.

Diagnosis: The Food Coloring Test

Add 5–10 drops of food coloring to the tank water (not the bowl). Wait 15 minutes without flushing. If the colored water appears in the bowl, your flapper is leaking. If the water stays in the tank, the problem is likely the fill valve or float.

Fix #1: Replace the Flapper (Most Common)

The flapper is the single most common cause of a running toilet. Made of rubber, it degrades over time — becoming warped, stiff, or coated with mineral deposits — and stops creating a watertight seal.

  1. Turn off the water supply valve behind the toilet and flush to empty the tank.
  2. Unhook the old flapper from the overflow tube (it usually has two ears that hook onto pegs).
  3. Disconnect the chain from the flush lever.
  4. Take the old flapper to the hardware store to find an exact match (or buy a universal flapper, which fits most toilets).
  5. Install the new flapper, reconnect the chain, and turn the water back on.

A replacement flapper costs $3–$8 and the entire job takes about 10 minutes. This single repair fixes a running toilet roughly 70% of the time.

Fix #2: Adjust the Float

If the flapper is fine but water keeps running, the float may be set too high, causing water to continuously overflow into the overflow tube. The fix depends on your float type:

  • Ball float (on an arm): Bend the arm downward slightly so the ball sits lower. This causes the fill valve to shut off sooner.
  • Cup float (on the fill valve): Locate the adjustment screw or clip on the float and lower it by about half an inch. Test and adjust until the water level sits about one inch below the top of the overflow tube.

Fix #3: Replace the Fill Valve

If adjusting the float does not work, the fill valve itself may be worn out. Signs include constant hissing, erratic water levels, or failure to shut off even with the float adjusted correctly.

  1. Turn off the water and flush the tank to empty it. Sponge out remaining water.
  2. Disconnect the water supply line from the bottom of the tank.
  3. Remove the locknut holding the fill valve in place (underneath the tank).
  4. Pull the old fill valve out and insert the new one.
  5. Tighten the locknut, reconnect the supply line, and turn the water on.
  6. Adjust the float height per the new valve's instructions.

A replacement fill valve costs $8–$15 and takes about 20 minutes to install.

The Chain Length Matters

A detail many people overlook: the chain connecting the flush handle to the flapper must be the right length. Too short, and the flapper cannot seat properly (causing a leak). Too long, and it can get caught under the flapper or fail to lift it fully during a flush. Leave about half an inch of slack in the chain.

Summary

Fixing a running toilet is a 10–20 minute job that costs under $15 in parts. There is genuinely no reason to call a plumber for this repair — and no reason to let thousands of gallons of water go to waste.
