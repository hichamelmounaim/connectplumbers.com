Water Heater Maintenance: The Complete Annual Checklist

Extend your water heater's life by years with these simple maintenance tasks.

Your water heater is one of the hardest-working appliances in your home, running 24 hours a day, 365 days a year to deliver hot water for showers, dishes, and laundry. A standard tank water heater costs between $800 and $2,000 to replace (including installation), and the average lifespan is 8 to 12 years. But with proper annual maintenance, you can push that lifespan to 15 years or more — and improve efficiency along the way.

Here is the complete annual maintenance checklist every homeowner should follow.

1. Test the Temperature and Pressure Relief (TPR) Valve

The TPR valve is the most important safety device on your water heater. It opens automatically to release pressure if the water temperature or tank pressure exceeds safe levels, preventing a potential explosion. Testing it takes 60 seconds:

  1. Place a bucket beneath the discharge pipe connected to the TPR valve.
  2. Lift the valve lever gently for 5 seconds, then release.
  3. You should hear a rush of water into the bucket. If the valve does not release water, or if it continues to leak after you release the lever, the valve needs to be replaced immediately.

A faulty TPR valve is a serious safety hazard. If the valve does not work, call a plumber immediately.

2. Flush the Tank to Remove Sediment

Over time, minerals (primarily calcium and magnesium) settle at the bottom of the tank, forming a layer of sediment. This sediment reduces heating efficiency, causes popping and rumbling noises, and can accelerate tank corrosion. Flushing the tank removes this buildup:

  1. Turn off the gas or electricity to the water heater.
  2. Connect a garden hose to the drain valve at the bottom of the tank. Route the hose to a floor drain or outside.
  3. Open the drain valve and let the water flow until it runs clear (usually 3–5 minutes).
  4. If the water is heavily discolored, turn the cold water supply back on briefly to stir up remaining sediment, then drain again.
  5. Close the drain valve, disconnect the hose, and turn the water supply and heater back on.

3. Inspect the Anode Rod

The anode rod is a sacrificial metal rod (usually magnesium or aluminum) that hangs inside the tank. Its purpose is to attract corrosive minerals in the water so they eat the rod instead of the tank lining. Once the anode rod is fully corroded, the tank itself starts to rust — and once a tank rusts through, it cannot be repaired.

To inspect the anode rod:

  1. Turn off the power and close the cold water supply.
  2. Locate the anode rod port on top of the tank (usually a hexagonal bolt).
  3. Use a 1-1/16 inch socket wrench to unscrew and remove the rod.
  4. If the rod is less than half an inch thick, or if it is coated with calcium deposits, replace it.

Replacement anode rods cost $20–$40 and are one of the best investments you can make in extending your water heater's life.

4. Check the Temperature Setting

The Department of Energy recommends setting your water heater to 120°F (49°C). This temperature is hot enough for comfortable showers and effective cleaning, while reducing the risk of scalding and saving energy. Every 10°F reduction saves 3–5% on water heating costs.

Many water heaters ship with the thermostat set to 140°F, which is unnecessarily high for most households.

5. Inspect for Leaks and Corrosion

Do a visual inspection of the entire water heater:

  • Check all pipe connections for moisture or drips.
  • Look for rust or corrosion on the tank body, especially at the bottom.
  • Inspect the area around the tank for signs of past leaks (water stains, mineral deposits).
  • For gas water heaters, inspect the flue and vent pipe for gaps, corrosion, or blockages.

6. Insulate the Tank (If Older)

If your water heater is more than 10 years old and is warm to the touch, it could benefit from an insulation blanket. These cost about $20 and can reduce standby heat loss by 25–45%, saving $20–$50 per year on energy costs.

Modern water heaters typically have adequate built-in insulation, so check the manufacturer's recommendations before adding an external blanket.

When to Replace Instead of Repair

If your water heater is over 10 years old and showing any of these signs, replacement is usually more cost-effective than repair:

  • Rust-colored water from hot taps
  • Visible rust on the tank body
  • Frequent need for repairs
  • Insufficient hot water despite proper settings
  • Pooling water around the base of the tank

Annual maintenance is the most cost-effective thing you can do for your water heater. One hour of work per year can save you thousands in premature replacement costs.
