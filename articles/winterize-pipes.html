How to Winterize Your Pipes and Prevent Freezing

Frozen pipes can cause thousands in damage. Here is exactly how to protect your home.

Every winter, hundreds of thousands of homeowners deal with the nightmare of frozen pipes. When water freezes inside a pipe, it expands with tremendous force — up to 2,000 pounds per square inch — enough to rupture copper, PVC, and even steel pipes. A single burst pipe can release hundreds of gallons of water into your home, causing catastrophic damage to walls, floors, ceilings, and personal property. The average insurance claim for water damage from frozen pipes exceeds $10,000.

The good news? Preventing frozen pipes is straightforward and inexpensive. Here is a complete winterization checklist.

Understanding Which Pipes Are at Risk

Not all pipes are equally vulnerable. The pipes most likely to freeze are:

  • Exposed pipes in unheated spaces: Basements, crawl spaces, attics, garages, and utility rooms.
  • Pipes along exterior walls: Especially on the north-facing side of the house where they receive less sunlight.
  • Outdoor hose bibs and irrigation lines: Any pipe that runs outside or through an uninsulated exterior wall.
  • Pipes in poorly insulated areas: Even interior pipes can freeze if the surrounding insulation is inadequate.

Step 1: Insulate Exposed Pipes

Pipe insulation is the single most effective defense against freezing. Foam pipe sleeves (available at any hardware store for about $3–$5 per 6-foot section) slide over exposed pipes and provide a thermal barrier. For irregular shapes or tight spaces, use fiberglass pipe wrap or heat tape.

Focus on pipes in crawl spaces, attics, garages, and along exterior walls. Even pipes that have never frozen before can freeze during an unusually cold snap, so err on the side of insulating more rather than less.

Step 2: Disconnect and Drain Outdoor Hoses

  1. Disconnect all garden hoses from outdoor faucets (hose bibs).
  2. Drain the hoses completely and store them indoors.
  3. If your hose bibs have interior shut-off valves, close them and open the exterior faucet to drain any remaining water.
  4. Consider installing frost-proof hose bibs, which have a long stem that places the shut-off valve inside the heated envelope of your home.

Step 3: Seal Air Leaks

Cold air infiltration is a major contributor to pipe freezing. Inspect your home for gaps, cracks, and openings near pipes — especially where pipes penetrate exterior walls or the foundation. Seal gaps with caulk, spray foam insulation, or weatherstripping. Pay special attention to:

  • Gaps around pipes where they enter the house
  • Dryer vents and exhaust fans
  • Basement rim joists
  • Electrical and cable outlets on exterior walls

Step 4: Keep the Heat On

If you are leaving your home for an extended period during winter, never turn off the heating completely. Set your thermostat to at least 55°F (13°C). This may seem wasteful, but it is far less expensive than dealing with burst pipes and water damage.

Open cabinet doors beneath kitchen and bathroom sinks to allow warm air to circulate around pipes against exterior walls.

Step 5: Let Faucets Drip During Extreme Cold

During severe cold snaps (when temperatures drop below 20°F / -7°C), open vulnerable faucets to a slow, steady drip. Moving water is much harder to freeze than standing water, and the slight flow relieves pressure buildup inside the pipe — which is what actually causes pipes to burst.

You only need to drip faucets that are fed by exposed or vulnerable pipes. A slow drip costs pennies per day but can prevent thousands of dollars in damage.

Step 6: Drain Sprinkler and Irrigation Systems

If you have an in-ground sprinkler system, have it professionally blown out with compressed air before the first freeze. Residual water left in irrigation lines will freeze, expand, and crack the pipes and fittings — leading to expensive repairs in the spring.

What to Do If a Pipe Freezes

If you turn on a faucet and only a trickle comes out, a pipe may be frozen. Do not panic. Here is what to do:

  1. Keep the faucet open so that as ice melts, water can flow and relieve pressure.
  2. Apply gentle heat to the suspected frozen section using a hair dryer, heat lamp, or towels soaked in hot water. Start at the faucet and work back toward the frozen area.
  3. Never use an open flame (blowtorch, propane heater) to thaw pipes. This is a serious fire hazard and can damage the pipe.
  4. If you cannot locate the frozen section or if the pipe has already burst, shut off the main water supply immediately and call a plumber.

The Bottom Line

Winterizing your pipes takes one afternoon and costs under $50 in materials. Failing to do so can cost you $10,000 or more in emergency repairs, water damage restoration, and insurance deductibles. It is one of the highest-return maintenance tasks any homeowner can perform.
